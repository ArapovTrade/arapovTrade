The method the program is built around

Volume analysis by the Wyckoff method — reading the market through volume and the balance of supply and demand, without indicators, where the goal is to see what large capital is doing and stand on its side. Richard Wyckoff described four market phases: accumulation, markup, distribution, markdown. All the learning on the site revolves around that logic.

Why this matters for the reading order. Indicators are derivatives of price, they lag and on their own forecast nothing (I set VWAP aside, that is a volume benchmark). Chart patterns, in my experience, work out roughly fifty-fifty, no more than pictures. So in the program technical analysis comes not as an end in itself, but as the language in which volume is later read. First you learn to see levels and structure, then behind that structure you start to see the intent of the large player.

Where to start and why exactly in this order

You start not with patterns and not with indicators, but with how the market works: who trades on it, how price forms, what a level is. The main beginner mistake, I say this often, is trying to forecast the market right away. Forecasting is a thankless job. Far more useful is a simple skill: learning to see a bounce off a level and a break of a level. That can be picked up in weeks and already pays off, while guessing the future is never mastered at all.

From there the layers stack one on another. Trading basics and the Forex market give the frame. Technical analysis, Japanese candlesticks and support and resistance levels teach you to read the chart. Volume analysis and the Wyckoff method add the main dimension, volume. And only on that foundation does Smart Money make sense, otherwise it turns into a set of pretty words. First see, then enter, and so at every level of difficulty.

If you would rather not pick topics by hand, follow a ready route. Each one runs from simple to complex, and every next step rests on the previous.

Intermediate level (6–12 months)

Here you stop looking at candles in isolation and start reading the volume and intent behind them. That is the turning point: the chart stops being a set of figures and becomes a story of supply fighting demand.

Stage 1, deeper technical analysis: candlestick patterns, pin bar, engulfing, head and shoulders, triangles, Fibonacci levels.

Stage 2, volume analysis: volume analysis, Wyckoff method, Wyckoff volume concepts, peak volume levels, order book analysis.

Stage 3, introduction to Smart Money: Smart Money guide, liquidity pools, order blocks, imbalance and FVG, false breakouts.

The nine sections of the program

If you prefer to move by topic rather than by route, here are the nine sections. Under each, briefly: what it is for and where to start in it.

Smart Money Concept (14 articles)

This is about reading the traces of large capital: banks, funds, market makers. Important point: without a base in volume and levels this section is too early to take, otherwise order blocks and liquidity pools turn into magic. Start: Smart Money guide, order blocks, liquidity pools.

Market volume analysis (18 articles)

The core of the method. Reading the market through volume, the phases of accumulation and distribution, the order book. Honest volume, for me, is the exchange volume of CME futures, not tick volume, and that is an important caveat when choosing instruments. Start: Wyckoff method, volume analysis.

Technical analysis (28 articles)

The largest section and at the same time the foundation of the chart's language. But treat patterns soberly: they have to match the market phase. The same flag that runs beautifully in a markup fails again and again in distribution, so a pattern on its own is not a signal, it works only at a strong level. Start: Japanese candlesticks, support and resistance levels.

Fundamental analysis (12 articles)

The calendar, Fed rates, gold, oil, indices. For a retail trader this is market context, a backdrop, not the main entry tool. The trend is set by fundamental factors, but the entry point is still found on the chart. Start: fundamental analysis, economic calendar.

Cryptocurrencies (22 articles)

Crypto basics, bitcoin and ether, market mechanics and, most important for a beginner, safety and scam protection. The rule here is simple: not your keys, not your coins. Start: crypto basics, how to start.

Trading for beginners (39 articles)

The largest block: getting started, market structure, risk management, trading systems. If you are a beginner, this is exactly where to start, not with a dozen random YouTube videos each selling a different truth. Start: trading basics, beginner mistakes.

Trader's glossary (7 articles)

Trading psychology (10 articles)

This section is worth plugging in not at the end but from the very start. What drains accounts is more often emotion than lack of theory: chasing fast money, FOMO, tilt, the urge to win it back after a loss. Start: how emotions affect trading, tilt.

Breakdowns of real situations on the chart, where theory comes together into a single entry and exit. This is the bridge between reading articles and the real chart, so it is useful to watch once you have the base down. Start: practical examples.

Psychology and risk: why from day one

Risk control is the foundation of trading, not the final chapter. You do not trade without a stop-loss: systemic risk will sooner or later zero the account, it is only a matter of time. For myself I keep the risk small per trade and place the stop before I size the position. This is not advice for you personally, just how I do it.

Psychology runs alongside. You can know the method and still drain the account if a losing streak catches you in tilt and you start averaging down. So the psychology section in the program is not a separate final course, but a line running through everything: emotion and risk accompany every level of learning, from the first trade to building a system.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to learn trading?

Not quick. In my experience, reaching consistent trading takes years of practice, not months, and most timelines you see online are optimistic. But the first useful skill, seeing a bounce and a break of a level, is picked up in weeks. After that it comes down to discipline, not speed.

 Can you teach yourself trading for free?

Yes. All the theory on the site and the videos on the channel are free, no limits. The catch with self-study is order: a structured curriculum beats random videos. Going alone is where many people lose extra years, I lost a couple myself on books and indicators.

 Is trading hard to learn?

The concepts are not hard. Holding to them under real money is. Most people drain accounts not from a lack of theory but because emotion breaks the plan on a losing streak. That is why risk and psychology run through the program from day one, not at the end.

 Should I learn forex or futures first?

Pick one and master it before branching out, do not split your attention. The method of volume and levels reads both the same way. I work mostly futures and gold, but start with whatever is closer and more accessible to you, just do not jump between markets every week.

 Do I need a demo account?

It helps for warming up the mechanics without risking money. But do not sit on it too long: a demo has no emotion, and emotion is what drains a live account. Once you reliably see a bounce and a break of a level, move to a small real size.
