Features of the Cryptocurrency market

Hello everyone! My name is Igor Arapov, and today we will continue our discussion on cryptocurrencies. Today, I want to talk about the unique aspects of the cryptocurrency market compared to other markets.

The first thing I want to highlight is the profitability of the cryptocurrency market, which is its volatility. The price of a coin can increase by hundreds or even thousands of percent in a day, especially if it's a meme coin. This doesn't happen in regular regulated markets. This volatility scares conservative investors but attracts those who enjoy taking risks, much like in a casino, hoping to get rich quickly. But that's not our focus today. Today, we'll discuss three key aspects of the cryptocurrency market, particularly Bitcoin: liquidity, volatility, and market capitalization. We'll talk about what they mean and why it's important to understand them.

First, let's look at the order book. Here, you can see that trading volumes on the market are low. Today is Sunday, but on any other day, the volumes will be about the same. Maybe slightly more or slightly less, but overall, these are small volumes. We won't consider iceberg orders — these are algorithmic orders that hide their full size. All of this is done by robots, not humans. We only take into account the numbers we see.

These numbers indicate that the market is illiquid. An illiquid market means that you can't buy or sell large amounts without affecting the price. Money on such a market will inevitably impact the price. This is a significant problem for large capital and one of the reasons why big money is cautious about entering the cryptocurrency market. No one wants the price to move against them. Large banks have already bought Bitcoin during sell-offs because smart money always buys during a market sell-off. But if you look at the situation separately, you can see that the market is thin, meaning it's illiquid. This is the key reason for volatility. Volatility depends on liquidity. The market's capitalization is relatively small compared to the stock market and other conservative markets. So it all makes sense here: no liquidity — expect volatility.

Now let's talk about market capitalization. Capitalization is calculated simply: the number of coins is multiplied by the current price. If the market is illiquid, it means that large volumes can't be sold or bought without changing the price. Market capitalization is very conditional. In the order book, small portions of coins are traded. The price is just the last completed transaction. Volumes show how much can realistically be bought and sold, and when they are low, it is a problem for the market. That's why large capital buys assets during sell-offs to create conditions for price growth and attract as many participants as possible to join the bull trend. The buying demand driven by news and greed allows large players to sell without significantly changing the price against their sales.

The market has phases of accumulation by large capital and distribution. If you are not familiar with them, watch my videos on volume analysis of the market. It's important to understand that on a thin market, professionals will try to lure in as many participants as possible to sell their positions. Until they do this, you can be confident in the continuation of the bull trend because you can't sell much on such a market without changing the price. Certain conditions need to be created, which is what large players do to sell their assets. Knowing this, you can comfortably work between the phases of accumulation and distribution, enjoying the volatility.
