Anatomy of market trends

Hello everyone! Today, we will talk about how trends work according to the Wyckoff method. In previous articles, we have already discussed that the market has two phases: accumulation and distribution. Smart money buys cheaply during the accumulation phase and sells at a higher price during the distribution phase, profiting from this. Today, we will understand what happens inside these phases and how to use this information to make a profit.

First of all, the scheme I will show you today is classic. About 80 years ago, Richard Wyckoff formed this structure. At that time, there was no Forex, no futures, no computers, and no mobile phones. People traded on traditional exchanges, where life moved more slowly. Today, technology has significantly sped up our lives, and the exchange operates much faster. Now, every trader has the necessary terminals on their phone, and an order can be executed in fractions of a second.

However, there are aspects that will never change as long as the market exists, and aspects that can be ignored because they are no longer as relevant.

The first thing to understand is that there are points on the chart where smart money will try to buy. They do this quickly and in large quantities, as much as the market allows. This is an element of greed: they want to buy cheaply to later sell at a higher price in an inefficient market. These points are called value areas. The market profile methodology, developed by Peter Steidlmayer, defines value areas as zones of maximum trading volume.

For large players, it is important to find zones where they can buy without moving the price. In the past, this happened slowly, but now time is a variable in the "price + volume" formula. Large capital still needs price and volume, but the time it takes to obtain this liquidity can vary.

Now let's analyze the anatomy of the market. Large players need liquidity, and price determines where it is profitable for them to buy or sell. During the accumulation phase, a large player buys, and when liquidity runs out, the trend changes. Large players buy cheaply and sell at a high price, but they do not sell during this time.

When the market is active, time does not play a significant role. For example, if there has been a strong decline, large players can quickly buy, and the price will shoot up. In another situation, activity may be lower, and accumulation will take more time. But the essence remains the same: large money needs liquidity.

It is also important to understand that volumes in the cryptocurrency market may be fake. This is a problem since there is no regulation, and it is difficult to understand how honest everything is. Therefore, always check volumes for common sense.

These orders form the value area where large players can place their orders. If all the parameters align, then the selling value area, where the crowd is actively selling, becomes the buying area for large players. This will lead to a price increase, indicating that the asset is undervalued.

I hope this is clear. Now let's define liquidity. If we have an accumulation phase where a large player was buying, sooner or later, liquidity will run out, and the trend will change. Large players buy cheaply and sell at a high price, and they need liquidity for this. When the market is in strong hands, there can be no talk of a decline. There may be local declines, but the trend will continue.
