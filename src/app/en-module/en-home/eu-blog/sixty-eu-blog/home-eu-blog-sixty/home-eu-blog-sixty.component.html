Trading Vs Options comparison of instruments

Hello, friends! My name is Igor Arapov, and today, by popular request from my subscribers, I’ll talk about binary options and trading. I should note that in my 11 years of trading, I’ve never traded binary options. When I was starting out, I was told that in most cases, they don’t work out, so I decided not to waste time on them. Therefore, my response will be a logical analysis based on what I found using the G Mini neural network (just Google) by searching "binary options."

So, binary options are a financial instrument that allows traders to bet on whether the price of an asset will rise or fall within a certain period of time. It’s similar to trading, but with the added condition of time.

Trading is challenging on its own, and here you need not only to predict the market but also to do it within a specific time frame, which complicates things. For example, in regular trading, you can buy the euro-dollar pair and set a stop-loss, expecting the price to move in your favor. This is straightforward and relatively simple. But if you need the euro to rise on a specific day or week, it becomes more difficult.

Next, I found information that you need to choose an asset, direction, and expiration time for the option—ranging from a few minutes to a few days. Personally, as a trader, this feels uncomfortable to me. It adds another layer of complexity to forecasting.

The outcome is this: if your prediction is correct, you get a fixed profit. But the problem is that you earn less than what you risk. In trading, we control risk using stop-losses and aim to earn more than we risk. But with binary options, you’re risking more than you can potentially earn. I don’t like that.

And if you look at the statistics on binary options traders, I think it becomes clear why the conditions are unfavorable. Overall, the terms of trading binary options seem to work against us and in favor of the exchange.
