Divergence on indicators

Hello, friends! I'm Igor Arapov, and today I will talk about market overbought and oversold conditions.

First of all, I want to say that I view this topic with irony, as it is related to indicators. The most popular of them is the Relative Strength Index (RSI). I've already explained why I don't use such indicators, but considering their popularity, I think it's important to express my opinion. Perhaps, after this, someone will reconsider using these indicators.

Firstly, the main task of oscillators like RSI is to determine when market assets are overvalued or undervalued. The indicator itself is not that simple. It uses data from the last 14 bars to determine the range. Values above 75 are considered overbought (a signal to sell), and below 25 are considered oversold (a signal to buy). Thus, the indicator tries to explain its complex calculations.

Sometimes traders also look for divergences — situations where the price is rising, but the indicator is not. If divergence works, the indicator succeeds in prediction.

However, I want to say that divergences don't always work. Overbought and oversold are very relative concepts, especially when viewed through the eyes of an indicator limited to 14 bars. To make sense of this, it's better to use levels. Don't complicate your life — if there is momentum, just define the levels and that's it. Why do you need an indicator to understand, for example, that it's not worth selling the euro right now?

I think that beginners use indicators because of their psychology. They are afraid to make their own decisions because the market often shows them that they are wrong. It's easier for them to believe in indicators. If they have an indicator signal, they can say: "That's why I'm in a drawdown, it's not my fault, I was just following the indicator." Recognize yourself? I did that too.

Indicators give the illusion of justified decisions, but the result is often disappointing. If they really worked, I wouldn't be telling you about complex methods like volume analysis or Smart Money strategies. We would just use indicators and follow their signals. But reality is more complicated.

Market prices are determined by common sense, your eyes, and your brain. If you shift responsibility to indicators, you are doomed to fail. You can blame the indicators all you want, but it won't help.

So, we've discussed divergences that sometimes work and sometimes don't. Here's an example: in 2013-2014, the euro was worth about 1.4 dollars per euro. Then the ECB started lowering the rate, and the euro fell to parity. RSI was constantly showing oversold conditions, but the euro kept falling. This greatly undermined my trust in the indicator. Therefore, I advise not to rely too much on such things.
