Algorithmic Orders on the Exchange

Hello everyone! My name is Igor Arapov, and today we will talk about algorithmic orders. In one of my previous publications, I discussed the types of orders and their impact on price. Today, we will delve deeper into this topic to learn about the advantages of large market participants over regular traders and make useful conclusions.

To begin with, I asked an AI to gather information about the types of algorithmic orders on the “CME” exchange. The “CME” exchange is a transparent, regulated exchange, and all information about it is honest and verified.

It is important to note that market orders are executed by small participants 95% of the time, who are just starting to trade. They only have two buttons with their broker - buy and sell - which are executed at the best available price. These traders usually do not use other types of orders, whereas there are many algorithmic orders that help conceal the final order.

More experienced traders understand that there are large players in the market. For success, it is important to monitor their actions, as large participants usually have a better understanding of the market. They use clever algorithmic orders to hide their intentions from small traders. When executed, they always conceal the size of their order.

We pay attention to volume because it shows the number of orders executed. The information in the “order book” shows intentions, but it is difficult to trust because large players can "play" the “order book,” hiding the true size of their orders.

Algorithmic orders are aimed at execution at a specific price. Price levels and volume are important because large players buy or sell at certain levels. If an asset is expensive, a large player will not buy, and the price will fall. If an asset is cheap, big capital will buy, but it will have to push the price up due to limited liquidity.

Conclusion:

Large players use all technologies to ensure their orders are executed quickly and discreetly, with minimal impact on price. The retail trader cannot compete with them and often uses market orders.

If we know that the task of big capital is to fill their order at a certain price, we must wait for them to do so. We will see spikes in volumes and activity at levels. When liquidity disappears, we can carefully join in.

Key takeaway:

Levels work if used correctly. Simply buying at a level is not always a successful strategy. Before a price reversal, there may be fluctuations that can lead to losses. It is important to know when to buy from a level.

In the “Smart Money” concept, a false breakout is a signal to act. Big capital accumulates liquidity, knocking out the stops of those who joined the movement. They create a shakeout before the movement begins, as stops are located nearby.

A retail trader can always buy and sell in the market because the market maker provides liquidity. You need to wait for the right level, see the activity of big capital through volumes, see the liquidity shortage and manipulations, and join in. This will help avoid market manipulations and set stops correctly.
