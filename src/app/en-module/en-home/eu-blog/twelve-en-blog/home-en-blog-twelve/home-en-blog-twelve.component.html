Trading of levels

Hello everyone! My name is Igor Arapov, and today I will talk about trading levels for beginner traders. I will explain how to draw levels, how to use them, and what they mean.

What is a Level?

First, let's understand what a level is. A level is a place on the chart where the price has changed direction. For example, the price was rising, reached a certain point, and then sharply turned downward. We can draw two lines across this range, showing that the market met resistance at this point and reversed. The sharper and more impulsive the price change, the higher the quality of the level. To draw good levels, you need to find points on the chart where there has been a sharp price change. Such levels can be considered high-probability and used for trades.

Rules for Working with Levels

The first thing to understand is that a level reflects value. Market participants, forming levels, determine what is considered expensive and what is cheap in the market. The market moves between these two values. As long as market participants consider one price to be expensive and the other to be cheap, the price will fluctuate between these levels. A support level acts as support when the price turns upward and as resistance when the price turns downward.

Ways to Work with Levels

  • Bounce from the Level: traders work within the range, from level to level.
  • Breakout of the Level: support is broken, sellers take control of the market, and after a retest, a downward movement forms.

Now let's consider how to work within the range and during level breakouts.

Working Within the Range

If we have identified a support level, remember that we work from support towards purchases until we see a breakout. Until the breakout occurs, we consider the level as support and do not sell into it, as this contradicts statistics. In most cases, the level repels the price, and a breakout occurs less frequently than a bounce. Therefore, it is better to work within the range on a bounce from the level.

The goals in such an analysis are simple: if two key levels are identified — cheap and expensive — you can work from one level to another without predicting additional scenarios. We are only concerned with the current value shown by the market. If the level is not broken, you can continue trading in this range until the market is influenced by fundamental factors, such as news or central bank statements.

Breakout of the Level

A breakout always starts impulsively. This is the first factor indicating a breakout. Market participants working from purchases fix losses, which is accompanied by increased volumes. The price sharply goes down but almost always returns for a retest of the level. In 99% of cases, a retest occurs. Traders should work based on the statistics of trades, not on each individual trade, so skipping a few trades due to the absence of a retest is not critical.

In conclusion, I want to say that levels are key price ranges where the price reverses. You should work from level to level until a breakout occurs.
