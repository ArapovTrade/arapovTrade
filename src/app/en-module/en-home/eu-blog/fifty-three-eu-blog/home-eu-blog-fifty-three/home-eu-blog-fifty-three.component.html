Block Order in Trading

Hello everyone! My name is Igor Arapov, and today we will talk about order blocks. This is a very popular topic on the internet, and I want to share my thoughts on it.

Order block is a part of the “Price Action” methodology, where we try to find large orders that can influence the price. The name speaks for itself: an order block is something large. In this video, we will discuss what it looks like and how it can be used.

First of all, I have a volume indicator on my chart. This will help us understand how an order block forms because it's hard to see just by looking at the price alone. Volume is a reaction to price, and somewhere at a certain price point, there will be a large order waiting to be executed.

Large market participants want to sell without affecting the price. For example, selling gold. We see how the order block pushed the price down. A large participant would prefer to sell without moving the price, but an order block forms where there isn't enough demand. Therefore, large orders have to be executed at market prices, driving the price down.

Order block is a clever algorithmic order that is executed in a special way, and it takes experience to spot it. It doesn't form often because most large orders are executed with limit orders to avoid impacting the price. An order block is an exception that is usually seen between major levels when the new price level is far off and large capital has no choice but to push the price through the market.

Order block starts with the first bar that sets the direction of the order. At its end, it touches the buyers. The algorithmic order hits the order book, reaches the nearest support level where there are buy orders, and these orders gradually push the price up. Then the market is tested under this demand for a while until it weakens, and the order begins to aggressively and rapidly push through the market.

This is a clever algorithmic order, and it takes practice to recognize it. I provided an example on gold where this is clearly visible. It's a rare occurrence, but if you see it, make sure to use it, as order blocks significantly change the price and are executed where there are no counterparts. In this case, there is not enough demand, and the order block is forced to push the price down. The price drop increases the number of sellers, and a mass of orders drives the price even lower.
