Scalping is a popular trading strategy often used by beginners. In this video, I'll discuss its nuances.

First, let's define what scalping is. It's when a trader quickly enters the market, makes a profit, and immediately closes the trade. The problem is, the faster you want to make money, the harder it is. Making a profit in short time frames is very challenging.

Many traders are not prepared for this type of trading. Now, I'll show you some simple math to understand a scalper's net profit. Look at the commission percentage and ask yourself: are you ready to continue trading this way? Most beginner scalpers don't set stop orders. This is wrong. Without risk control, there's no point in being on the market. Without stops, you're just waiting to lose your account.

Now let's do the math. I drew two examples: a scalper and a day trader. Let's assume we bought an asset at 1, the price rose to 10, and we locked in a profit of 9 points. But we have a broker who takes a commission. Whether you trade in profit or loss, the commission is always there. This creates a negative mathematical expectation in the market. If there were no commission, the probabilities would be distributed 50/50. But with the commission, your account is likely to be wiped out with a 99.97% probability.

Let's say the commission is 2 points, and you earned 9. Net, you have 7 points left. It seems okay, but if you calculate the commission percentage, it's about 18% of your profit. In day trading, it's 20%. If you have a systematic income, from the entry point to setting a stop loss and exit, and your system gives a positive mathematical expectation, you can use it for a long time.

But achieving this in short time frames is difficult. Profit in quick trades is only available through high-frequency arbitrage, which requires expensive technology. The ideal scalping setup is impossible for retail traders. Finding such a system is extremely difficult. Most traders don't set stops because the market might stop them out. This is especially challenging for retail traders.

Now, an example of a scalper: he bought an asset at 1, the price rose to 5, he locked in a profit of 4 points, but gave 2 points to the broker. That's 50% of your net profit. Why should the broker get half of your profit? This is the main problem with scalping.

If you have everything systematic and professional, you can live and be happy. But for retail traders, it's a trap. A scalper is an excellent client for the exchange because he feeds it. By trading with your money, you are essentially feeding the exchange and the broker. Your money goes to the market and the broker. That's why it's important to reduce commissions. Ideally, to 5 points.

If you're a beginner and don't set stops, paying 50% of your profit to the broker creates a negative mathematical expectation. If your system has a negative mathematical expectation, your business is doomed. Commissions are the reason for the negative mathematical expectation. You need to minimize them as much as possible or avoid trading scalping.
