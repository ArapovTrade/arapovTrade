How to read Japanese candlesticks?

Hello everyone! Today we will talk about Japanese candlesticks. I will explain which candlestick patterns I use in trading and which ones I do not. My name is Igor Arapov, and I have been trading for over 11 years. I will try to explain everything in a simple and understandable way.

All beginner traders get acquainted with Japanese candlesticks because it is the most popular type of chart. Almost all books on trading and technical analysis use candlesticks. There are many reversal formations and patterns, but not all of them are useful. Today I will tell you what is really useful and what is not worth your time so that you can develop faster and more efficiently in trading.

On the chart (more details about the chart https://youtu.be/58JiqJj9Gs4?si=LaqJAHAq3XfVFCHD) you see Japanese candlesticks. A candlestick can be bullish or bearish. Usually, bullish candles are green, and bearish candles are red. Candles have parameters: open price (left), close price (right), high, and low price. If the close is higher than the open, the candle is green; if lower, it is red. This is the basis of Japanese candlesticks.

Formations

Now about formations. In my experience, most formations do not work. They often work with a probability of 50 to 50. Therefore, I do not recommend wasting time on them. But what is really important is volume analysis, which complements candlestick analysis.

Volumes show the number of transactions over a certain period and help to understand the struggle between supply and demand. Volume analysis allows you to see market reversals that are not always visible on candlesticks.

Useful Aspects of Japanese Candlestick Analysis

Now let's talk about the useful aspects of Japanese candlestick analysis. There are two levels on the chart, and we will discuss the 1-2-3 rule. This is a standard rule in technical analysis that will improve the quality of your trades.

1-2-3 Rule

  • Point one – this is the start of the wave.
  • Point two – this is the peak of the wave.
  • Point three – this is the key point for entering a trade, a correction to the 1-2 wave.

At point three, we look for formations such as a "hammer" or "pin bar". These long-tail candles indicate a possible reversal. For example, a hammer or pin bar at a level is a signal for entry. You have a clear place to set a stop-loss because the price cannot be below the level.

Conclusion

Use the 1-2-3 rule and look for "hammer" or "pin bar" formations at levels. If your trade's ratio exceeds 1 to 2, you will grow your deposit. Do not be afraid of stop-losses; they control risks and protect your deposit.
