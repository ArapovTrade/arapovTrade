Averaging in trading

Hello, friends! My name is Igor Arapov, and today we’re going to talk about averaging in trading—how to do it correctly, the risks involved, and how to manage those risks.

Averaging is a straightforward concept. Imagine you bought one Bitcoin at $10,000. Then the price drops to $5,000. This often happens to beginners who buy at the peak. Now you’re facing a $5,000 loss. You might think, "If I buy another Bitcoin at $5,000, my average purchase price drops to $7,500." That way, you need a smaller price increase to break even.

It sounds logical, but in practice, averaging against the trend often leads to bigger losses. Why is that?

First, if you’re trading without risk control, you’re already in a dangerous position. Trading without a stop-loss—and averaging is essentially trading without a stop-loss—is a risky strategy. Even when averaging, you need to have a stop-loss in place. When you average down against the trend, you’re buying into a weakening market. If the market continues to drop, you could face even greater losses.

Markets can change quickly. For example, shifts in monetary policy can cause a currency to devalue rapidly. Take the euro/dollar, which dropped from 1.4 to parity in just 13 weeks. In such cases, averaging won’t save you. Trends can last much longer than traders expect, and if you plan to average, you would need to start with very small positions to avoid running out of funds—an approach that’s not very efficient.

That’s why averaging against the trend is generally a bad idea. It’s a high-risk move that can wipe out your account. As a standalone trading strategy, averaging should not be used. The only time averaging makes sense is when you’re following the trend, and even then, it should be done with a stop-loss in place. Trading with the trend gives you a positive mathematical expectation, and averaging can enhance your returns in such cases. It’s a tool for increasing profits, but if you trade against the trend, your account will quickly diminish. If you trade with the trend, your account can grow quickly. However, managing your risks is crucial.

These are the key principles for working with averaging and the most important risks to be aware of. I recommend that beginners avoid using this strategy. Averaging is best left to experienced traders and should only be done in line with the trend. Beginners should focus on trading with clear stop-losses and managing risks carefully. It’s important to first learn how to understand the market and improve your ability to make accurate predictions. Since trading is all about probabilities, you need to be aware that increasing your risk—averaging is one way to do that—can be very costly.
