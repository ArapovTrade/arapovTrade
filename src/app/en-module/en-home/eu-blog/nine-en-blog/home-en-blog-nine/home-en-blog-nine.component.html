How to predict the market price?

Hello everyone! My name is Igor Arapov, and today we will talk about how to forecast market prices. In previous articles, I mentioned that beginner traders need to learn to see bounces from levels or breakouts. Now I will delve deeper into this topic and discuss what beginners are often interested in. Unfortunately, many of them get confused with their goals and therefore do not achieve success.

What do I mean?

When we draw levels on the chart, we see where the market considers the price to be cheap or expensive. At cheap levels, we look for entry points for buying. How exactly to find these points — by volumes or candlestick patterns — is a secondary question. The main thing is to understand that the market is telling us that the price does not want to go lower. We interpret this information and buy, placing a stop-loss. This is not related to price forecasting.

When large funds and banks forecast a company's profit, they do not use indicators like stochastic or MACD. Therefore, beginner traders hoping to find a tool that will tell them what the market will do in a day or two fall into a trap. Forecasting future trends is a complex task that requires a large amount of information and experience.

Those who try to forecast prices using indicators are essentially looking at the past. But why do you need indicators when you can simply identify levels, see where it is expensive, where it is cheap, and trade between these levels? Intraday traders simply listen to the market and act accordingly. This does not guarantee 100% success, but it increases the likelihood of a positive outcome. Over time, you will be in profit if you follow the risk-reward ratio.

All trading comes down to monotonous activity — buying at a level and selling at a level. Attempting to forecast the market is unrealistic; it is impossible. On my YouTube channel (www.youtube.com/@ArapovTrade), I record weekly market reviews, discuss news and factors affecting the market, mark levels, and try to forecast fundamental things such as the Fed's monetary policy. But any event can change the forecast.

A trader should not clutter their mind with forecasts. They open the chart, look at the levels, and act. If you explain your role in the market correctly, there will be no problems: buy where it is cheap, sell where it is expensive. Trying to forecast the price a few days ahead will lead to problems. This is too complex a task. It is better to trade from level to level — anyone can do this.

Start with simple things, as I recommend in the "Quick Start". Grow gradually, having a solid foundation, but do not rely on indicators for market forecasting. Start with levels, and you won't need to engage in forecasting.
