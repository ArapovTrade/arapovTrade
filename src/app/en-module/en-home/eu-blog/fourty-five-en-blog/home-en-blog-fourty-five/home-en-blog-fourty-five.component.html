How to use moving averages in Trading?

Hello everyone! My name is Igor Arapov. Today we will discuss “moving averages”. If you regularly follow my publications, you know that I do not particularly respect this type of indicator. In my 11 years of practice, I have not found them useful, although for some traders they are, for others not. But since my blog is educational, and many beginner traders have not yet fully understood this topic, it is important for me to cover it.

First, let's talk about what “moving averages” are. This is an indicator that shows the trend direction — up or down — depending on the period it is calculated for. For example, I have two moving averages here: one with a period of 21 and another with a period of 50. The period is the number of consecutive bars considered in the calculations. The longer the period, the slower and more significant the changes in the indicator, because the data updates less frequently.

Let's look at an example. If we have a small period, like 9 bars, the indicator responds quickly to price changes. If the period is large, like 200 bars, the indicator changes slowly and shows the overall price trend.

It is also important to note that “moving averages” are used in investments to determine the trend in long-term markets, such as stocks. For example, investors can use a 200-day moving average to determine whether a stock is trending up or down. They buy stocks if the price is above the moving average, otherwise, they wait.

However, in trading on short-term markets, such as Forex or futures, moving averages can be less effective due to the fast-changing price. Their use can slow down decision-making and lead to missed opportunities.

In conclusion, moving averages have their place in slow markets and long-term investments, but for active trading, I recommend studying other indicators, such as “volumes” and “pin bars”, which can be more effective for decision-making in fast and volatile markets.
