The Law of Expected Value

In our world, there are laws, such as the law of gravity. People do not fly not because they do not want to, but because gravity does not allow it. In trading, there is an analogous law — the law of expected value. The market by default has a negative expected value due to the spread. This means that for every dollar of risk, you earn slightly less, for example, 0.98 cents. This small deviation is enough to leave you without a deposit over a long distance.

A trader who wants to make money must change this expected value. Today, we will discuss how to do this. If you can change the expected value in your favor, you will trade successfully and not repeat the mistakes of most traders.

For example, I asked an AI to calculate the probability of 100 trades with a 33% success rate and a risk/reward ratio of 1 to 3. This means that for every dollar of risk, you earn three dollars. The AI calculated that the average expected result per trade would be 0.32 cents. This means that out of 100 trades, your average profit will be 32 dollars. These simple calculations highlight something important: trading is not guessing, but a game of probabilities.

When you trade randomly, you have a negative expectation. Even a small deviation, such as minus one cent, is enough to lose your entire deposit over a long distance. Our task as traders is to make the average expected result positive.

In the "Quick Start Trading" course, I talked about trading levels. We wait at levels and look for trading opportunities. This helps change the expected value. By default, the market gives us a negative expected value, but by setting our trading parameters, we can get a positive expected value. Then everything will be fine for the trader.

For successful trading, you need to follow conditions where for every dollar of risk, there are three dollars of profit. Even if the success rate is not 50/50 but 33%, you will still be in profit over a long distance. The main thing is to maintain the risk/reward ratio.

Many beginner traders focus on the probability of success, trying to find trading systems with almost 100% probability. Such systems do not exist. On average, it is 70/30 if you follow the rules of technical, volume, and candlestick analysis. This is enough to be in profit. It is important to maintain the risk/reward ratio.

When you make a decision to enter a trade, you must see that the movement to the resistance level should be at least 1 to 3. If the risk/reward ratio is less, you do not trade. This ensures that you will be in profit over a long distance. Mathematics guarantees this.
