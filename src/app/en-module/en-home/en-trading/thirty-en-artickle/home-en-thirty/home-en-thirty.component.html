Imbalance and FVG (Fair Value Gaps): How to Identify Strong Liquidity Zones?

What are Imbalance and FVG?

In modern trading, the concepts of Imbalance and Fair Value Gaps (FVG) have become important tools for liquidity analysis and identifying key market zones. These concepts help traders find areas where the price tends to return to balance after a sharp movement.

Definition of Terms:

  • Imbalance – a mismatch between supply and demand that leads to rapid price movement without significant counter volume.
  • Fair Value Gap (FVG) – a gap between candles that forms a price gap, which is not immediately filled. Such zones are considered strong liquidity levels.

Understanding these concepts allows traders to find key entry and exit points based on market movement patterns.

How Do Imbalance and FVG Occur?

Demand and supply imbalance (Imbalance) is a market condition where one side (buyers or sellers) dominates, causing a sharp price movement. This occurs when there is a lack of liquidity at certain levels, forcing the price to move rapidly without significant counter volume.

Fair Value Gaps (FVG) are gaps in price movement that arise due to rapid quote changes without proper testing of the price range. These zones are considered important liquidity levels because the market often returns to them to balance supply and demand.

Main Causes of Imbalance:

  • Sharp institutional buying or selling: When large participants (Smart Money) place massive orders, the price instantly shifts due to a lack of opposite orders.
  • News and economic events: Macro data releases, Federal Reserve meetings, and other major events cause sharp price jumps, leading to the formation of Imbalance and FVG.
  • Manipulation by large players: Smart Money creates artificial demand or supply, triggering level breakouts, after which the price returns to the liquidity zone.
  • Algorithmic trading: High-frequency trading bots can create instant price changes, causing Imbalance by instantly depleting liquidity at certain levels.

How Are Fair Value Gaps Formed?

Fair Value Gaps form as a result of aggressive price movement when a gap appears between three consecutive candles. This indicates that at a certain level, there were no counter orders to balance liquidity.

How to Identify FVG on a Chart?

  • Bullish FVG: If a gap forms between the low of the first candle and the high of the third candle, it is considered an unfilled liquidity zone.
  • Bearish FVG: If a gap remains between the high of the first candle and the low of the third candle, it indicates a seller imbalance.
  • Partially Filled FVG: Sometimes the market partially tests the gap but does not fully close it, leaving a potential entry point.

Why Are FVG Important Levels?

The market seeks balance, and unfilled liquidity zones act as a magnet for the price. Smart Money uses FVG to manage their positions, making them key levels for traders. In most cases, the price returns to these zones before continuing its main movement.

How to Find Strong Liquidity Zones Using Imbalance and FVG?

For effective liquidity analysis, it is important to identify areas where the price is most likely to return and test the levels left by Smart Money. Strong liquidity zones are areas on the chart where large orders or unfilled price gaps are concentrated, often becoming key support and resistance levels.

Why Does Liquidity Concentrate in Certain Zones?

Large players (institutional investors, market makers) cannot open or close their positions immediately, as this would cause a sharp market movement. Therefore, they use an accumulation strategy, creating areas with high trading volume, which later become liquidity zones. These levels help traders find entry points with minimal risks.

Methods for Finding Strong Liquidity Zones:

  • Analyzing FVG on the daily chart: Daily gaps often serve as strong support and resistance levels, as the price tends to fill them.
  • Combining Imbalance with volume analysis: Zones with high trading volume usually indicate potential retest points where the price may slow down or reverse.
  • Using cluster charts: Order flow analysis (Footprint Charts) helps to see where the market is overloaded with liquidity and identify potential reversal zones.
  • Comparing FVG on different timeframes : The most significant liquidity gaps form on larger timeframes (H4, D1, W1), making them key reference points for trading.
  • Identifying key levels based on price reaction: If the market has tested a certain level multiple times without breaking it, this may indicate hidden liquidity.

How to Interpret Liquidity Zones?

When analyzing Imbalance and FVG, it is important to consider not only their presence but also the price reaction to these levels. If the price quickly moves away from the imbalance zone without returning, it may indicate a strong impulse. However, if the market repeatedly returns to the FVG, it may suggest liquidity absorption before a reversal.

Smart Money often uses Imbalance and FVG zones to place large orders, so properly identifying them helps traders find entry points with a high probability of success.

Trading Strategies Using Imbalance and FVG

Imbalance and FVG are powerful tools for understanding market structure and identifying strong trading opportunities. They help traders determine levels where the price is most likely to slow down or reverse. Let's explore several key strategies that can be used in trading.

1. Trading FVG Retests

One of the most reliable methods is trading Fair Value Gaps (FVG) retests. When the price forms a liquidity gap, it often returns to this range before continuing its movement. This gives traders an opportunity to enter a trade with low risk.

How to use the strategy:

  • Identify FVG on timeframes such as H1, H4, or D1.
  • Wait for the price to return to this area and confirm with a signal (e.g., a candlestick pattern).
  • Place a stop-loss beyond the FVG boundary and set a take-profit in the direction of the main trend.

2. Entering a Position Using Imbalance

When Imbalance occurs at key levels (such as near major supports or resistances), it confirms the strength of the movement. In such situations, traders can look for entry points in the trend direction.

How to apply:

  • Identify Imbalance areas on the chart using volume analysis.
  • On a retest of the level, wait for a confirmation signal, such as increasing volume or a bullish/bearish pattern.
  • Enter a position in the direction of the dominant force (buyers or sellers).

3. Using FVG in Combination with Key Levels

When FVG coincides with important support or resistance levels, the likelihood of a bounce significantly increases. This happens because the price tends to fill the liquidity gap and test the area where there was previously an imbalance between supply and demand.

How to apply:

  • Look for FVG that intersects with historical support and resistance levels.
  • Wait for confirmation of the price reaction at this level (e.g., a bullish engulfing pattern or pin bar).
  • Enter a trade in the expected direction with a tight stop-loss.

4. Combining Imbalance and Volume Analysis

Additionally, traders can use volume analysis (Volume Profile, Footprint Charts) to determine real interest from large market participants. This helps filter out false signals and enter trades only in truly strong liquidity zones.

How to apply:

  • Identify Imbalance zones and check the volume in these areas.
  • When the price returns to the Imbalance area, observe the reaction of market makers.
  • If the price shows increasing volume and an acceleration in movement, this is a signal to enter a trade.

Using Imbalance and FVG in combination with proper risk management allows traders to minimize losses and increase the accuracy of market entries. The key is to be patient and wait for confirmation signals before making trading decisions.

Advantages and Disadvantages of Using Imbalance and FVG

Advantages:

  • Precision of levels: FVG helps identify strong support and resistance levels where the price may reverse or continue its movement.
  • Ease of use: Even beginner traders can apply these concepts, as they are easy to identify on the chart and provide clear signals.
  • Works on all timeframes: Suitable for both intraday and medium-term trading, allowing market analysis at any scale.
  • Effectiveness when combined with volume analysis: Imbalance and FVG provide the most accurate signals when volume and market clusters are considered.
  • Adaptability to different markets: These concepts work not only in the cryptocurrency market but also in forex, stock, and commodity markets.

Disadvantages:

  • Not always effective: Sometimes the market may ignore Imbalance and FVG zones, especially when new movement drivers appear.
  • Requires additional analysis: To improve accuracy, traders should use volume, order flow, and price behavior in these zones.
  • Cannot be used as a sole indicator: Without confirmation signals (e.g., candlestick patterns or volume analysis), FVG may generate false signals.
  • Risks for beginners: Some traders may misinterpret FVG and enter the market without additional confirmation.

Examples of Using Imbalance and FVG in the Real Market

Let's look at a few real examples where traders can apply these concepts to find entry points. Imbalance and FVG are formed during sharp price movements and can be used as reference points for identifying liquidity zones and potential reversal points.

1. Imbalance Before a Strong Trend

At the beginning of a trend, large candles often form without significant corrections – this is a signal of a strong imbalance between supply and demand. In such cases, the price tends to return to the Imbalance zone to compensate for the lack of liquidity.

Example:

  • On the hourly BTC/USD chart, a sharp rise is observed, triggered by large purchases.
  • An unfilled zone (FVG) forms where the price did not linger.
  • A few hours later, the market makes a correction to this area before continuing its upward movement.

2. Correction to the FVG Zone

When the price returns to an unfilled liquidity zone (Fair Value Gap), it creates an opportunity to enter a position in the direction of the main trend. These zones often act as support or resistance.

Example:

  • On the 4-hour S&P 500 chart, a strong upward impulse is observed.
  • After breaking a key resistance level, an FVG zone is formed.
  • A few days later, the price returns to this area, forming a reversal pattern.
  • This confirms liquidity restoration before the trend continues.

3. Trading Imbalance Retests

If the market creates an imbalance (Imbalance) after a strong movement, the price may return to this area for testing. This provides an opportunity to enter a trade with minimal risk.

Example:

  • On the EUR/USD chart, a bearish impulse is observed, accompanied by high volatility.
  • An unfilled area is formed where the price did not linger.
  • When the market returns to this zone, a reversal pattern appears, confirming the downtrend.
  • Entering a trade on the retest of the Imbalance zone allows for profit from the continuation of the downtrend.

4. Combining FVG with Volume Analysis

When FVG is confirmed by high volume, the probability of a bounce from this zone significantly increases. It is important to monitor the price reaction when approaching the Fair Value Gap.

Example:

  • A sharp price surge is observed in the gold market (XAU/USD).
  • On volume clusters, it is evident that liquidity is accumulating in the FVG zone.
  • As soon as the price retests this area, an increase in volume is observed.
  • This confirms interest from large players, which may lead to the continuation of the uptrend.

Analyzing Imbalance and FVG in combination with other technical analysis tools helps traders identify strong liquidity levels and make more informed trading decisions.

Conclusion

The concepts of Imbalance and Fair Value Gaps allow traders to identify strong liquidity zones and understand where large players may enter the market. By using these tools in combination with volume analysis and key levels, traders can significantly improve the accuracy of their forecasts and trading efficiency.

To succeed in trading, it is important not only to identify FVG and Imbalance zones but also to understand the context of market movements, price reactions at key levels, and volume behavior. This will help avoid false signals and effectively use market manipulations to your advantage.

If you want to improve your trading skills, start analyzing Imbalance and FVG zones across different timeframes, tracking how the price reacts to these levels. Over time, you will better understand market structure and use these concepts for more profitable trading.
