Leverage Risk in FOREX

Leverage is a type of credit provided by a broker against the collateral of funds deposited into a trading account. This credit can only be used for buying and selling currency and allows transactions in amounts exceeding one's own funds. Nowadays, leverage sizes range from 1:1 (one to one) to 1:500 (one to five hundred). The number following the colon indicates how many times the broker's credit funds can exceed one's own funds (deposit) during trading operations. Thus, with a deposit of $1000 and leverage of 1:200, one can operate an amount of $200,000, and with leverage of 1:500 — an amount of $500,000. However, this also increases the risk of losing the deposit completely. For example, with a deposit of $2000 and leverage of 1:200, one can buy or sell one standard lot.

Economic policy is determined by the interests of industrial and financial groups, exporters and importers of goods and capital, etc. Moreover, considering the increasing interdependence of countries worldwide, optimal economic policy represents a compromise between national and foreign interests.

The main economic factors that significantly influence currency exchange rate dynamics are economic indicators, negotiations, meetings of central banks, economic and trade unions, speeches by leaders of major financial and economic state institutions, specialists in economics and financial operations, and agreements on joint interventions.

  • — previous level

Additionally, the behavior of other markets should be considered: the stock market, gold market, oil market, and agricultural commodities market. If the central bank replenishes reserves by purchasing securities for resale, the discount rate will decrease, allowing banks to lower interest rates and causing the currency rate to fall. Conversely, reducing the country's reserves by selling securities for future buyback leads to an increase in the discount rate, making loans more expensive and enhancing interest in savings in national currency, strengthening it.

In case of rising oil prices, for oil-importing countries, an increase in demand for the US dollar can be expected, leading to its appreciation. Lower gold prices increase the acquisition of securities, leading to their appreciation and, consequently, strengthening the national currency. Similarly, cheaper gold raises interest in deposits, allowing banks to control interest rate growth and increase demand for the national currency.

For a more accurate assessment of the impact of a particular indicator on currency exchange rate dynamics, it is necessary to consider recent changes, specialists' forecasts, and the correlation of various indicators. It is often the specialists' forecasts that provoke the market's reflective reaction, which, upon data announcement, either intensifies (if the forecast is confirmed) or reverses (if the forecasts are incorrect).
