Currencies and their Quotations

The exchange rate, quotation, or price of a currency is determined by the market itself, i.e., by the ratio of supply and demand for the currency of a particular country. The quotation expresses the amount of foreign currency given for one unit of the national currency (reverse quotation) or, conversely, the amount of national currency that must be given for one unit of foreign currency (direct quotation).

Base Currency

The currency, the price of one unit of which is expressed in several units of another currency, is called the base currency. The main reserve currency in the world is the dollar, so in most quotations, it is the base currency.

Bid and Ask Prices

Major market participants post rates for both selling and buying, and the quotation becomes two-sided. For example: 1.5020 — 1.5025. At the left quotation, you can sell the base currency to a bank or broker, and at the right, you can buy it. The price at which the market participant posting the quotation is ready to buy the currency is called "bid" (BID), and the price at which they are ready to sell it is called "ask" (Ask) or "offer" (Offer). The difference between buying and selling is called the "spread" (Spread) and serves to profit those who post the quotation. The smallest possible change in the quotation is called a point or (in professional jargon) a pip. Different currencies are quoted with different precision and spread. Most currencies are quoted and change to the fourth decimal place, and the market spread is usually between 5 and 15 points.

Standard Currency Designations

Each currency has a standard designation accepted worldwide and fixed by the global ISO classification. The designation consists of three letters, the first two of which indicate the country, and the third — the name of the currency. For example, the US dollar, according to the classification, is designated as USD, where US stands for United States, and D is the first letter of the currency name Dollar.

  • EUR Euro
  • JPY Japanese Yen
  • GBP British Pound Sterling
  • CHF Swiss Franc
  • CAD Canadian Dollar
  • AUD Australian Dollar
  • NZD New Zealand Dollar
  • Greenback, Buck
  • Cable
  • Swissy

Example of Quotation

In its final form: a correctly constructed quotation takes the following form: USD/CHF 1.7450/1.7455

This quotation means that we can sell 1 US dollar for 1.7450 Swiss francs or buy 1 US dollar for 1.7455 Swiss francs.
