Behavioral Risks

Risk of Panic

During a sharp change in exchange rates, panic may arise as many market participants (especially small speculators) start to panic and spontaneously close their positions to avoid losing profit or to reduce losses. Such actions lead to even greater changes in the exchange rate. This creates a snowball effect: the more positions are closed, the more the price changes, and the stronger the panic becomes. Simultaneously, many participants begin to open positions in the direction of the price movement, hoping to quickly make a profit, and the exchange rate continues to move rapidly.

A sharp change in exchange rates can be caused by various factors such as the release of important financial news or the actions of large players, such as national banks of different countries. They influence the change in exchange rates by selling or buying large volumes of currency. Sharp changes in exchange rates can generate rumors that have no real basis.

To reduce such risks, it is recommended not to open trades during the period of expecting important news releases. It is important to constantly analyze the movement of the exchange rate to understand what caused this movement, use protective orders (take-profit and stop-loss), trade according to your own system, and strictly adhere to it.
